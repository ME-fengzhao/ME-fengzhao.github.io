Many estimation and control problems involve a process of the form \[ y_k=\phi_k \theta, \] where \(k =0, 1, 2,...\) is the discrete-time step corresponding to the continuous-time step size \(T_s\), the scalar or vector \(y_k\in \mathbb{R}^p\) is the measurement at step \(k\), the matrix \(\phi_k \in \mathbb{R}^{p\times n}\) is the regressor at step \(k\) whose entries consist of current and past data, and \(\theta \in \mathbb{R}^{n}\) is a column vector of \(n\) unknown parameters. The objective is to use \(y_k\) and \(\phi_k\) to estimate the components of \(\theta\). In applications, \(y_k\) and \(\phi_k\) are corrupted by noise, and thus it does not hold exactly. This motivates the need for the least squares estimates of \(\theta\) given below.

The measurements \(y_k\) and the data in \(\phi_k\) are typically obtained from a continuous-time process and, as such, are available at the sample times \(kT_s\), where \(T_s\) is the sample interval. The batch approach to this problem is to collect a large amount of data and then apply least squares optimization to the collected data to compute an estimate of \(\theta\). In particular, collecting data over the time window \(i =0,...,k\), it follows that \[ \Upsilon = \Phi \theta, \Upsilon=[y_0, ..., y_k]^\top, \Phi =[\phi_0, ..., \phi_k]^\top \] Note that the above equation has the form \(Ax =b\), where \(A\) denotes \(\Phi\), \(x\) denotes \(\theta\), and \(b\) denotes \(\Upsilon\).

In the presence of noise corrupting the data \(\Upsilon\) and \(\Phi\), it may not have a solution. In this case, it is useful to replace it by a least squares optimization problem of the form: \[ \begin{aligned} J_k(\hat \theta) =& \sum_{i=0}^k (y_i-\phi_i \hat \theta)^\top(y_i-\phi_i \hat \theta) + (\hat \theta-\theta_0)^\top R(\hat \theta-\theta_0) \\ =&(\Upsilon -\Phi \hat \theta)^\top(\Upsilon -\Phi \hat \theta)+(\hat \theta-\theta_0)^\top R(\hat \theta-\theta_0) \end{aligned} \] where \(R\) is a positive semidefinite (and thus, by definition, symmetric) matrix, and \(\theta_0\) is an initial estimate of \(\theta\). In particular, the batch least squares (BLS) is given by \[ \theta_{opt,R}=(\Phi ^\top\Phi+R)^{-1} (\Phi^\top \Upsilon+R\theta_0) \] Note that the inverse is of size \(n \times n\), and thus the computational requirement of the inverse is of order \(n^3\). In addition to the inverse, three matrix multiplications are needed. Note also that the memory needed to store \(\Phi\) grows with \(k\). Furthermore, if \(\Phi\) has full column rank, then \(R\) can be set to zero, and it becomes \[ \theta_{opt,0}=(\Phi ^\top\Phi)^{-1} (\Phi^\top \Upsilon) \] In many applications, computational speed and memory are limited. One way to alleviate these requirements is to recursively update an estimate of \(\theta_{opt,R}\) using each additional measurement \(y_k\). A recursive algorithm of this type is especially convenient for real-time applications.

Recursive least squares (RLS) is a technique used for minimizing a quadratic cost function, where the minimizer is updated at each step as new data become available. RLS is more computationally efficient than batch least squares, and it is extensively used for system identification and adaptive control. This article derives RLS and emphasizes its real-time implementation in terms of the availability of the data as well as the time needed for the computation.

RECURSIVE LEAST SQUARES

This section provides a statement and proof of the RLS algorithm. This result involves a recursive algorithm for optimizing \(J_k\) at each step \(k\). The optimization of \(J_k\) updates the estimate \(\theta_k\) of \(\theta\) as measurements and data become available. We include a forgetting factor \(\lambda\), which provides higher weighting to more recent measurements and data.

Theorem 1:

For all \(k \geq 0\), let \(\phi_k \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times n}\), and \(y_k \in \mathbb{R}^{p}\). Furthermore, let \(\theta_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{n}, P_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\) be positive definite, and \(\lambda \in (0, 1]\). For all \(k \geq 0\), denote the minimizer of the function \[ \begin{aligned} J_k(\hat \theta) =& \sum_{i=0}^k \lambda^{k-i}(y_i-\phi_i \hat \theta)^\top(y_i-\phi_i \hat \theta) + \lambda^{k+1}(\hat \theta-\theta_0)^\top P_0^{-1}(\hat \theta-\theta_0) \\ \end{aligned} \] by \[ \theta_{k+1}=\text{argmin}_{\hat \theta \in \mathbb{R}^n} J_k(\hat \theta) \] Then, for all \(k \geq 0\), \(\theta_{k+1}\) is given by \[ \begin{aligned} P_{k+1} = & \frac{1}{\lambda} P_k-\frac{1}{\lambda}P_k \phi_k^\top (\lambda I+\phi_k P_k\phi_k^\top)^{-1} \phi_kP_k\\ \theta_{k+1}=& \theta_k +P_{k+1} \phi_k^\top (y_k-\phi_k\theta_k) \end{aligned} \]

Three Useful Lemmas:

The following result is the quadratic minimization lemma:

LEMMA 1 :

Let \(A\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\), assume that \(A\) is positive definite, let \(b \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and \(c \in \mathbb{R}^n\), and define \(f:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\) by \[ f(x) = x^\top Ax +2b^\top x +c. \] Then the unique minimizer of \(f\) is \[ x_{opt}=-A^{-1}b, \] and the minimum value of \(f\) is \[ f(x_{opt})=c-b^\top A^{-1}b \] The following result is the matrix inversion lemma:

LEMMA 2 :

Let \(A\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}, U\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p}, C\in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p}, V\in \mathbb{R}^{p\times n}\), and assume that \(A,C\), and \(A +UCV\) are nonsingular. Then \[ (A +UCV)^{-1} = A^{-1}-A^{-1}U(C^{-1}+VA^{-1}U)^{-1}VA^{-1} \] LEMMA 3 :

Let \(A\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\) be positive semidefinite, let \(B\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\), and let \(C\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\) be positive definite. Then, \[ \left[I-AB^\top(C+BAB^\top)^{-1}B\right]AB^\top = AB^\top(C+BAB^\top)^{-1}C. \]

Proof :

Note that \(J_0 (\hat \theta)\) can be written as \[ \begin{aligned} J_0 (\hat \theta) = &(y_0-\phi_0 \hat \theta)^\top(y_0-\phi_0 \hat \theta) + \lambda(\hat \theta-\theta_0)^\top P_0^{-1}(\hat \theta-\theta_0) \\ =& (y_0^\top- \hat \theta^\top \phi_0^\top)(y_0-\phi_0 \hat \theta)+\lambda(\hat \theta^\top-\theta_0^\top)(P_0^{-1}\hat \theta- P_0^{-1}\theta_0) \\ =& y_0^\top y_0 -2y_0^\top\phi_0 \hat \theta+\hat \theta^\top \phi_0^\top\phi_0 \hat \theta +\lambda \hat \theta^\top P_0^{-1}\hat \theta-2\lambda \theta_0^\top P_0^{-1}\hat \theta+\lambda\theta_0^\top P_0^{-1}\theta_0 \\ =& \hat \theta ^\top \underbrace{(\phi_0^\top\phi_0+\lambda P_0^{-1})}_{A_0}\hat \theta +2 (\underbrace{(-\phi_0^\top y_0-\lambda P_0^{-1}\theta_0)}_{b_0})^\top \hat \theta+\underbrace{y_0^\top y_0+\lambda\theta_0^\top P_0^{-1}\theta_0}_{c_0} \\ =& \hat \theta ^\top A_0\hat \theta+2b_0^\top \hat \theta+c_0 \end{aligned} \] Defining \[ P_1=A_0^{-1}, A = P_0^{-1}, C = \frac{1}{\lambda}I, U=\phi_0^\top , V = \phi_0 \] With LEMMA 2, we have \[ \begin{aligned} P_1= & \frac{1}{\lambda}\left( P_0^{-1}+\frac{1}{\lambda}\phi_0^\top\phi_0 \right)^{-1}\\ =& \frac{1}{\lambda}P_0^{-1}-\frac{1}{\lambda}P_0 \phi_0^\top\left(\lambda I+\phi_0^\top P_0 \phi_0\right)^{-1}\phi_0P_0 \end{aligned} \] Additionally, because \(A_0\) is positive definite, it follows from Lemma 1 that the unique minimizer is given by \[ \begin{aligned} \theta_1 =&-A_0^{-1} b_0 \\ = & A_0^{-1}\phi_0^\top y_0+\lambda A_0^{-1}P_0^{-1}\theta_0\\ =& P_1\phi_0^\top y_0+\lambda P_1P_0^{-1}\theta_0\\ =& P_1\phi_0^\top y_0+ P_1(P_1^{-1}-\phi_0^\top\phi_0)\theta_0\leftarrow ({ \color{red}A_0/P_1^{-1}-\phi_0^\top\phi_0=\lambda P_0^{-1}})\\ =& P_1\phi_0^\top y_0+ \theta_0- P_1\phi_0^\top\phi_0\theta_0\\ =& \theta_0+P_1\phi_0^\top(y_0-\phi_0\theta_0) \end{aligned} \]

Hence, it is satisfied for \(k =0\).

Now, let \(k \geq 1\). Then \(J_k(\hat \theta)\) can be written as \[ \begin{aligned} J_k(\hat \theta) = & \hat \theta ^\top A_k\hat \theta+2b_k \hat \theta+c_k \\ A_k = & \sum_{i=0}^k \lambda^{k-i}\phi_i^\top\phi_i+\lambda^{k+1} P_0^{-1}\\ b_k = & -\sum_{i=0}^k \lambda^{k-i}\phi_i^\top y_i+\lambda^{k+1} P_0^{-1}\theta_0\\ c_k = & -\sum_{i=0}^k \lambda^{k-i}y_i^\top y_i+\lambda^{k+1} \theta_0^\top P_0^{-1}\theta_0 \end{aligned} \] Furthermore, \(A_k\) and \(b_k\) can be written recursively as \[ \begin{aligned} A_k= & \lambda A_{k-1} + \phi_k ^\top \phi_k \\ b_k= & \lambda b_{k-1} - \phi_k ^\top y_k \end{aligned} \] Defining: \[ P_{k+1}=A_k^{-1}, \] it follows from Lemma 2 with \[ A = P_k^{-1}, C = \frac{1}{\lambda}I, U=\phi_k^\top , V = \phi_k \] we have \[ \begin{aligned} P_{k+1} = & \left(\lambda (A_{k-1} + \frac{1}{\lambda}\phi_k ^\top \phi_k)\right)^{-1}\\ = & \frac{1}{\lambda}\left(P_k^{-1}+ \frac{1}{\lambda}\phi_k ^\top \phi_k\right)^{-1}\\ = & \frac{1}{\lambda}P_k^{-1}-\frac{1}{\lambda}P_k \phi_k^\top\left(\lambda I+\phi_k^\top P_k \phi_k\right)^{-1}\phi_kP_k \end{aligned} \] Furthermore, the minimizer \(\theta_{k+1}\) of \(J_k\) is given by \[ \theta_{k+1} = -A_k^{-1}b_k \] Because \(A_k\) is positive definite, it follows from Lemma 1 that \[ \begin{aligned} \theta_{k+1} =&-A_k^{-1} b_k \\ = & A_k^{-1}( \phi_k ^\top y_k-\lambda b_{k-1}) \\ = & A_k^{-1}( \phi_k ^\top y_k+\lambda A_{k-1}\theta_k)\leftarrow {\color{red}\theta_{k} = -A_{k-1}^{-1}b_{k-1} }\\ = & A_k^{-1}\left( \phi_k ^\top y_k+(A_k-\phi_k ^\top \phi_k)\theta_k\right)\leftarrow {\color{red}A_k= \lambda A_{k-1} + \phi_k ^\top \phi_k}\\ = & A_k^{-1}\phi_k ^\top y_k+ (I-A_k^{-1}\phi_k ^\top \phi_k)\theta_k\\ =& \theta_k+ A_k^{-1}\phi_k ^\top (y_k-\phi_k\theta_k) \\ =& \theta_k+ P_{k+1}\phi_k ^\top (y_k-\phi_k\theta_k)\leftarrow {\color{red}P_{k+1}=A_k^{-1}} \end{aligned} \] Hence, Theorem 1 is satisfied.

Note that, if \(\lambda =1\), \(R\) is positive definite, and \(P_0=R^{-1}\), then the RLS minimizer \(\theta_{k+1}\) is equal to the BLS minimizer \(\theta_{opt,R}\).

The notation \(\theta_{k+1}\) for the minimizer of \(J_k\) emphasizes the fact that \(\theta_{k+1}\), which is based on data available up to step \(k\), is not available until the update given by Theorem 1 is completed, which occurs at step \(k +1\).

The computational requirements of RLS are primarily determined by \(n\). In particular, \(P_k\) is \(n\times n\), and thus the computational requirement for updating \(P_k\) given by (9) is of order n2 . Furthermore, the inverse in Theorems 1 is of size \(p\times p\), which, since typically \(p \ll n\), is much less demanding than the inverse required by BLS. In addition, the storage requirements of RLS are of order \(n^2\), which does not grow with \(k\). Consequently, the computational and memory requirements of RLS are significantly less than those of BLS.

ALTERNATIVE \(\theta_k\) UPDATE WITH INVERSE

The following result is a variation of Theorem 1. In this formulation, the updates of \(P_k\) and \(\theta_k\) are reversed.

Theorem 2:

For all \(k \geq 0\), let \(\phi_k \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times n}\), and \(y_k \in \mathbb{R}^{p}\). Furthermore, let \(\theta_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{n}, P_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\) be positive definite, and \(\lambda \in (0, 1]\). For all \(k \geq 0\), denote the minimizer of the function \[ \begin{aligned} J_k(\hat \theta) =& \sum_{i=0}^k \lambda^{k-i}(y_i-\phi_i \hat \theta)^\top(y_i-\phi_i \hat \theta) + \lambda^{k+1}(\hat \theta-\theta_0)^\top P_0^{-1}(\hat \theta-\theta_0) \\ \end{aligned} \] by \[ \theta_{k+1}=\text{argmin}_{\hat \theta \in \mathbb{R}^n} J_k(\hat \theta) \] Then, for all \(k \geq 0\), \(\theta_{k+1}\) is given by \[ \begin{aligned} \theta_{k+1}=& \theta_k +P_{k} \phi_k^\top(\lambda I+\phi_k P_k\phi_k^\top) (y_k-\phi_k\theta_k)\\ P_{k+1} = & \frac{1}{\lambda} P_k-\frac{1}{\lambda}P_k \phi_k^\top (\lambda I+\phi_k P_k\phi_k^\top) \phi_kP_k\\ \end{aligned} \]

Proof :

Using \(P_{k+1}\) in Theorem 1, we have \[ \begin{aligned} \theta_{k+1}=& \theta_k +\left(\frac{1}{\lambda} P_k-\frac{1}{\lambda}P_k \phi_k^\top (\lambda I+\phi_k P_k\phi_k^\top)^{-1} \phi_kP_k\right) \phi_k^\top (y_k-\phi_k\theta_k) \\ =& \theta_k+\frac{1}{\lambda}\left(I-P_k \phi_k^\top (\lambda I+\phi_k P_k\phi_k^\top)^{-1}\phi_k\right)P_k\phi_k^\top (y_k-\phi_k\theta_k)\\ =& \theta_k+P_k\phi_k^\top(\lambda I+\phi_k P_k\phi_k^\top)^{-1}((y_k-\phi_k\theta_k) \leftarrow \color{red}\text{LEMMA 3 } \end{aligned} \]

REAL-TIME IMPLEMENTATION OF RECURSIVE LEAST SQUARES

In many applications, it is desirable to implement RLS so that the estimate \(\theta_k\) is available in real time without latency. Note that the estimate \(\theta_{k+1}\) depends on measurements available up to and including step \(k\), namely, \(y_k\) and \(\phi_k\) . Since time is needed to compute \(\theta_{k+1}\), the updated estimate \(\theta_{k+1}\) is not available at time \(k\); rather, it is available at the next step, namely, \(k +1\). Consequently, the minimizer of \(J_k\) is denoted by \(\theta_{k+1}\), where the subscript \(k +1\) conveys the fact that the minimizer of \(J_k\) is not available until step \(k +1\).
